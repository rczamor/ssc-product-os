# Who Buys Security Ratings, and Why

## Core buyer: third-party risk management (TPRM)

The anchor buyer is the CISO/GRC organization running vendor risk: continuously monitoring hundreds-to-thousands of suppliers where questionnaires alone don't scale. Adjacent internal buyers: procurement (pre-contract screening), boards and executives (a legible A–F benchmark of their own company vs. peers), and security operations (supply-chain incident response — the framing SecurityScorecard now brands "Supply Chain Detection and Response").

Market sizing (analyst-firm estimates vary widely by definition): Fortune Business Insights projects the TPRM market growing from ~$11.75B in 2026 to ~$38.39B by 2034 (~16% CAGR); SkyQuest sizes it at ~$9.5B (2024) growing to ~$37B by 2033. Ratings platforms are a slice of this; growth is consistently modeled at mid-teens CAGR across firms.

## Cyber insurance underwriting

Insurers and brokers use outside-in ratings for risk selection, application validation, subjectivity management, and pricing — SecurityScorecard markets this use case directly, and MarketsandMarkets lists Bitsight, SecurityScorecard, and UpGuard among top companies in the cyber insurance ecosystem. The structural tie is deep: Moody's put $250M into Bitsight (2021) and Marsh McLennan co-published Bitsight's incident-correlation study; the cyber insurance market itself is forecast to roughly double from ~$16.5B (2025) to ~$32B (2030) (MarketsandMarkets). For rated companies this raises the stakes of a bad grade: it can affect premiums or coverage, which fuels both adoption and resentment.

## M&A and financial due diligence

Private equity and corporate development teams run ratings on targets pre-close (fast, no target cooperation needed) and across portfolio companies post-close. Bitsight/Moody's explicitly position ratings for financial diligence; SecurityScorecard sells portfolio monitoring to PE. Ratings function here as a cheap screen that triggers deeper technical diligence.

## Regulatory pressure (the strongest current tailwind)

- **DORA** (EU, applies since Jan 17, 2025): financial entities must manage ICT third-party risk, maintain registers of ICT providers, and monitor them continuously — continuous vendor monitoring tooling maps directly onto this (DAC Beachcroft).
- **NIS2** (EU, transposition due Oct 2024 onward): extends supply-chain security obligations to "essential/important entities" across many sectors, with management-level accountability.
- **SEC cyber disclosure rules** (US, 2023): material-incident 8-K disclosure within four business days plus annual disclosure of risk-management programs, including oversight of third-party risk — pushing boards to want quantified, defensible vendor-risk evidence.
- Sector regimes (FFIEC guidance, UK/APRA equivalents) impose parallel expectations; vendors sell ratings as audit-ready evidence of "continuous monitoring."

## Analyst coverage

Forrester is the primary evaluator of the category: The Forrester Wave: Cybersecurity Risk Ratings Platforms, Q2 2024 assessed 10 vendors, naming SecurityScorecard, Bitsight, and Panorays Leaders (Black Kite and Recorded Future Strong Performers). Forrester also maintains the stance that ratings are "a valuable piece of the third-party risk puzzle" — an input, not a complete program (Forrester report RES179129). Gartner never built a Magic Quadrant for ratings; it covers them inside its Market Guide for Third-Party Risk Management Technology Solutions (2025 edition names RiskRecon and others) and via Peer Insights. Gartner's much-quoted 2018 prediction — "by 2022, cybersecurity ratings will become as important as credit ratings when assessing the risk of existing and new business relationships" (TechRepublic) — is now cited both by vendors (as vision) and skeptics (as an overshoot: ratings remain influential but are not credit-rating-grade institutions).

## Buying dynamics worth knowing

Deals are custom-quoted, sized by number of monitored vendors; free tiers (SecurityScorecard's free scorecard, Bitsight's complimentary report) seed land-and-expand. A recurring buyer objection: paying per-vendor monitoring fees for scores their own suppliers dispute as inaccurate — see ratings-industry-criticism.md.
