## Role Context

This person sits in SecurityScorecard's post-sales GTM org but lives in the pre-sales blast radius too: they are the hybrid CSM/Sales-Engineer who has the platform open on a shared screen more hours per week than almost anyone in the company. SecurityScorecard's own CSM job postings describe the role as guiding customers "from onboarding/implementation through upsell and renewal" and explicitly managing "the gross and net retention of their portfolio, including forecasting" (Built In / Greenhouse listings, base+bonus roughly $100K–$130K). Their book skews enterprise TPRM and CISO-office buyers; every account also has a free-tier surface (any organization can claim its own scorecard at no cost), which means the product is constantly being judged by non-customers who see their score before they ever see a salesperson.

## Goals

Keep GRR at or above ~90% and push NRR past 105% through module expansion (SCDR, MAX managed services — the company's fastest-growing product at 370% YoY); get every new logo to a first-value moment fast enough that the champion can defend the purchase internally; and never lose a renewal to a preventable cause: an unresolved score dispute, a stale champion relationship, or a QBR that couldn't prove ROI.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (expanded)

1. **Demo live without landmines.** Pull up a prospect's own scorecard in real time — the signature move of ratings sales — and have the data hold up under a hostile CISO's scrutiny.
2. **Onboard to first value fast.** Claim the customer's scorecard, load the vendor portfolio (Automatic Vendor Detection), tune alert noise, and get the first board-ready report out within the first 30–60 days.
3. **Drive habitual adoption.** Convert "logs in before the QBR" accounts into weekly-active teams using Score Planner, issue workflows, and portfolio alerts.
4. **Absorb and resolve score anger.** Every score drop triggers an email. Walk customers (and their angry vendors) through dispute/correction/appeal, and make the 48–72 hour resolution SLA feel real.
5. **Prove ROI quarterly.** Build QBRs on the DEAR pattern (deployment, engagement, adoption, ROI) with exportable evidence: score trend, issues remediated, vendors escalated, incidents avoided.
6. **Protect and forecast the renewal.** Maintain health scores, multi-thread past the single champion, and call the renewal number accurately.
7. **Expand and evangelize.** Land MAX/SCDR upsells, get the Seal of Trust badge on customer websites, and source references.

## Top Pain Points

- **False positives are their problem, not the data team's.** G2 reviewers repeatedly cite misattributed IPs/domains and cloud auto-scaling artifacts generating "thousands of false positives" — but it's the CSM who sits in the meeting where the customer says the product is wrong.
- **Score-change opacity.** When a score drops and the customer can't see *why* from the UI, the CSM has to reverse-engineer the delta before the escalation call.
- **Demo fragility.** Slow-loading scorecards, stale findings, or an unclaimed prospect domain with bad attribution can torch a bake-off against Bitsight or Panorays.
- **Reporting gaps.** Limited customization forces manual deck-building for every QBR and board cycle.
- **Single-threaded accounts.** Champion departure is a top churn trigger; the product gives them few native signals about stakeholder breadth.

## KPIs and How They're Measured

GRR/NRR are measured on the book quarterly against plan (SaaS medians: GRR ~90%, NRR ~102–106%; top quartile 95%/111%+ per SaaS Capital and Statisfy 2025 benchmarks). Adoption is measured in platform telemetry (WAU, portfolios, alerts actioned). Time-to-value is milestone-based onboarding completion. Health scores follow a weighted usage/support/sentiment/engagement model; renewal forecast accuracy is inspected in pipeline reviews. Dispute escalations are tracked to closure against the published 24-hour response / 72-hour score-adjustment commitments.

## A Day in the Life

8:30 — scan overnight score-drop alerts across the book; pre-write context for two accounts before *they* email. 9:00 — onboarding call: new logo's vendor portfolio import, alert thresholds. 10:30 — joint call with an AE: live scorecard walkthrough for a prospect's CISO; fields two "that IP isn't ours" objections. 12:00 — files a refute with evidence for a customer's load-balancer false positives; sets a 72-hour follow-up. 1:00 — builds QBR deck manually because the export doesn't match the customer's board template. 3:00 — health-score triage: one account red (champion left), starts multi-threading play. 4:30 — renewal forecast update; flags one at-risk logo tied to an unresolved dispute.

## Evaluation Lens: What "Good" Looks Like in the Platform They Demo and Drive Adoption Of

Good = the platform makes them look credible live: fast scorecard loads, accurate attribution, a self-explanatory score-change narrative ("what changed, why, what to do"), one-click board/QBR exports that match how CISOs report, an in-product refute flow with visible status and honored SLAs, onboarding checklists that drive first value in days not months, and adoption telemetry the CSM can see per account. Every feature is judged by one question: *does this shorten the path from "customer is skeptical" to "customer renews and expands"?*

## Red Flags (what product gaps make their job harder / cause churn)

Unexplained score swings; false positives the customer finds before we do; refutes that sit in "Under Review" past SLA; demo latency or stale data in bake-offs; reports that require manual rebuilds; no native visibility into account stakeholder/usage health; free-tier users whose first impression is a wrong score with no obvious remediation path; and expansion modules (SCDR/MAX) that demo well but require services-heavy setup the CSM ends up doing themselves.
