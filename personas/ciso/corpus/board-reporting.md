## The disclosure regime changed what board reporting is for

Since December 2023, US public companies must disclose a cybersecurity incident on Form 8-K Item 1.05 within four business days of determining it is material — the clock starts at the materiality determination, not discovery — and must describe cyber risk management, strategy, and governance annually in the 10-K under Regulation S-K Item 106 (PwC). This turned the CISO's board packet from an FYI into disclosure-support infrastructure: what the board hears must be consistent with what the company files, and the CISO now drafts materiality inputs jointly with the GC and CFO.

Filing behavior shows how contested the materiality call is. Per the Tyson Martin analysis of filings from December 2023 through May 2026: only 29 issuers filed under Item 1.05 while 50 disclosed voluntarily under Item 8.01; after the SEC's May 2024 Gerding statement that Item 1.05 is exclusively for incidents determined material, Item 1.05's share of cyber 8-Ks fell from ~72% to ~34%. Average time from detection to an Item 1.05 filing was 7.88 business days; half of Item 1.05 filers later amended. Enforcement is real but targeted: Flagstar Bancorp paid $3.5M for stating there was "no evidence of unauthorized access" while knowing data was exfiltrated, and four SUNBURST downstream victims (Unisys, Avaya, Check Point, Mimecast) paid a combined $6.9M.

## Boards are cyber-accountable but not cyber-literate

The Diligent Institute/Bitsight study of 4,149 public companies found only 5% have a cybersecurity expert on the board. Yet oversight structure measurably matters: companies with a cyber expert on the audit or a specialized risk committee averaged a security performance rating of 700 vs. 580 when the expert sat only on the general board (Harvard Law Forum summary). Companies with advanced security performance ratings delivered ~4x shareholder returns — 71% five-year TSR vs. 37% for basic performers. The practical consequence for a CISO: the audience is smart, non-technical, and increasingly personally attentive — so externally-validated, comparable artifacts (letter-grade ratings, peer benchmarks) carry disproportionate weight because directors can interpret them without translation.

## What the report itself should look like

Deloitte's board-reporting guidance and current practice converge on a stable dashboard of roughly five to seven KPIs, organized by risk domain, each with current status and quarter-over-quarter trend — board metrics describe exposure, posture trend, and alignment to risk appetite (enabling resource-allocation and risk-acceptance decisions), while operational metrics (patch counts, ticket volumes) stay out of the packet. Established cadence: a standing CISO slot on the audit-committee agenda, typically quarterly, plus out-of-cycle escalation paths for material incidents.

## Implications for a security ratings platform

The board wants: (1) one number/grade with a defensible trend line, (2) peer comparison the CISO can vouch for, (3) consistency between what's presented internally and what's externally visible to investors, insurers, and plaintiffs' counsel. A ratings platform that produces board-ready trend and benchmark exports directly serves the Item 106 governance narrative; a platform whose score moves on bad data creates a disclosure-consistency problem, not just an annoyance.
