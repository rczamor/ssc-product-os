## The retention math CS teams at security vendors are managed against

Benchmarks set the targets this persona carries. Statisfy's 2025–2026 roll-up puts median B2B SaaS NRR at 106% with top performers above 120%, and median GRR at 90% with the top quartile at 95%. SaaS Capital's 2025 survey of private SaaS companies (>$1M ARR) shows that for the $25K–$50K ACV band — a common security-ratings deal size — median NRR is 102%, top quartile 111%, bottom quartile 97%, and that NRR correlates positively with ACV. A security-ratings CSM holding a book to "90/105" (GRR/NRR) is therefore being asked to perform at or slightly above median, with expansion modules doing the NRR lifting.

Two operational findings matter for how the work is done. Statisfy reports that cutting average time-to-intervention on risk signals from 9 days to 2 days correlated with a 6-percentage-point GRR improvement over the following two renewal cycles — the argument for daily alert triage rather than QBR-driven check-ins. And multi-threading is a retention control, not a sales tactic: Saber's churn-prevention playbook data attributes 8% of churn root-cause directly to champion change, reports roughly half of accounts churning after champion departure, and shows structured rapid re-engagement cutting post-departure churn from 45% to 19%.

## Health scoring: the DEAR pattern

Gainsight's DEAR framework (Deployment, Engagement, Adoption, ROI) is the de facto standard structure for B2B health scores. Its logic maps cleanly onto a ratings platform: Deployment = scorecard claimed, vendor portfolio loaded, integrations live; Engagement = champion plus periodic executive contact; Adoption = weekly active use of issue workflows and alerts, not just pre-QBR logins; ROI = demonstrable score improvement and vendor-risk reduction. Gainsight's guidance is to start with 4–6 weighted metrics (an illustrative weighting: usage 40%, support trends 25%, sentiment 20%, executive engagement 15%), define green/yellow/red thresholds, and validate with CSMs before automating — a crawl-walk-run rollout rather than a big-bang model.

## What the vendor itself expects of the role

SecurityScorecard's own Senior CSM postings (Built In/Greenhouse) are explicit that the CSM "manages the gross and net retention of their portfolio, including forecasting," covers the customer lifecycle "from onboarding/implementation through upsell and renewal," and is expected to expand ARR via cross-sell — with total compensation listed around $100K–$130K (base plus bonus). This is a quota-adjacent role: the CSM is not a support function but a revenue owner whose leverage is adoption.

## Implications for evaluating the product

The product is doing its job for CS when it (a) surfaces risk signals fast enough to support ~2-day intervention, (b) exposes per-account adoption telemetry a CSM can read against a DEAR-style model, and (c) generates QBR-ready ROI evidence (score trend, issues remediated) without manual assembly. Gaps in any of the three convert directly into GRR risk, because the CSM's alternative is spreadsheet forensics before every renewal call.
