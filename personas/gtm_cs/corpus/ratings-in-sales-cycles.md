## The free scorecard is the top of the funnel

SecurityScorecard's GTM is structurally product-led in a way most security vendors' are not: per its pricing pages, any organization can claim its own rating at no cost, a free account includes 5 scorecards of the buyer's choosing, and instant.securityscorecard.com emails a company its current score on demand. The Trust & Transparency FAQ states the company continuously rates more than 12 million entities and that "any organization — at no cost — can always access and improve their security rating." Practically, this means the signature sales move — pulling up the prospect's *own* scorecard live on the first call — is always available, and it cuts both ways: a compelling "F in Network Security" creates urgency, but a misattributed finding hands the prospect's security team a reason to distrust the whole dataset before procurement even starts. The demo is only as good as attribution accuracy on a domain nobody has groomed.

## Ratings as a seller-side asset: badges and trust surfaces

Rated companies also use their score outbound. SecurityScorecard's Badges feature (Help Center) lets customers embed a "Seal of Trust" badge on their website that links to their public scorecard — turning the rating into marketing collateral for the customer's own sales cycles. This matters to the CS persona because badge placement is both an adoption milestone and a switching cost: a customer whose homepage advertises their scorecard is unlikely to churn quietly. It sits alongside the broader trust-center trend: TrustMind's analysis finds public trust surfaces deflect roughly 20–56% of inbound security questionnaires for SMB/mid-market sellers but only ~10–15% once Fortune 500/regulated-buyer exposure is meaningful — i.e., ratings and trust pages accelerate deals but don't remove the diligence stage for enterprise.

## Competitive bake-offs

Ratings deals are routinely head-to-head. Forrester's Wave for Cybersecurity Risk Ratings Platforms has repeatedly placed Bitsight and SecurityScorecard together at the top (with Panorays joining the leader group in the Q2 2024 evaluation, per BankInfoSecurity), and Bitsight's own comparison pages attack on data quality and analyst placement while SecurityScorecard counters on scan frequency, in-house data ownership (the Trust FAQ claims 99% of data owned in-house) and free access. In a bake-off, the CSM/SE's practical battleground is: whose score of the prospect looks more accurate *today*, whose false positives surface first, and who can explain a score delta live without escalating to an analyst.

## Adjacent buying contexts that show up in cycles

Two non-TPRM contexts recur in deals and renewals: cyber-insurance underwriting (SecurityScorecard's insurance use-case page cites calibration against insurer claims data and correlation work with the Marsh McLennan Cyber Risk Intelligence Center) and board reporting, where a single letter grade is the artifact executives actually circulate. Both give the GTM/CS person high-leverage proof points — and both raise the stakes of accuracy, since a disputed score can now affect a customer's premium or board narrative, not just their dashboard.
